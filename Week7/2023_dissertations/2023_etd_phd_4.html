    Doctor of Philosophy, The Ohio State University, 2023, Biomedical Sciences

    A tissue specific niche of epidermal stem cells maintains epithelial homeostasis by both renewing stem cells and producing differentiation-competent cells. Cell autonomous and environmental cues control epidermal stem-cell commitment towards renewal or differentiation. Limited in vitro models exist to study stem-cell renewal in genetically unaltered epithelial cells. The conditional reprogramming cell culture technique, which supports indefinite epithelial cell propagation and stem-cell marker expression in culture, suggested that epithelial cells cultured this way could renew stem cells. The inhibitor Y-27632, initially developed against Rho-associated kinases (ROCKs), drives conditional reprogramming when epithelial cells are cultured in the Rheinwald-Green, additive rich medium conditions. This implied that the Rheinwald-Green conditions were permissive to stem-cell renewal, although lacking the ability to promote stem-cell renewal on its own. We first investigated how Y-27632 promoted conditional reprogramming in human foreskin keratinocytes (HFKs), to study epidermal stem-cell renewal. We identified several key areas in which Y-27632 acted upon keratinocytes that might enable conditional reprogramming, including maintaining cell viability during trypsinization, promoting cell cycle progression, and specifically promoting stem-cell marker expression. We tested Y-27632's presumed mechanism in conditional reprogramming to inhibit the ROCKs. Conditionally reprogrammed cultures decreased ROCK activity, which correlated with proliferation and stem-cell marker expression. To directly test Y-27632's ROCK specific role in conditional reprogramming, we used a combination of CRISPR-Cas9-mediated ROCK knockout HFKs and a panel of other ROCK inhibitors. Single isoform deletion of either ROCK1 or ROCK2 was insufficient to decrease ROCK activity or to conditionally reprogram HFKs.
